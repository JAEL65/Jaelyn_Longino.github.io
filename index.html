About Me

I am a student researcher analyzing global climate models. My work focuses on evaluating atmospheric model resolutions, cloud microphysics setups, and convective cycles across regional bounding boxes in the Central United States.

Research Abstract

The Impact of Model Resolution and Physics on Fixing the Central U.S. Dry Bias

Precipitation is notoriously difficult to accurately represent in Global Climate Models (GCMs). In particular, summertime precipitation over the central United States, much of which is associated with severe convective storms, has long been underestimated. To better understand and address the central United States dry bias, we examine the impact of atmospheric model resolution and cloud microphysics using various configurations of NOAA Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory’s (GFDL) AM4 model forced by observed sea surface temperatures. Preliminary results show that increasing atmospheric resolution from 100 to 50 to 25 km monotonically reduces mean precipitation biases over the central United States. However, introducing a two-moment cloud microphysics scheme did not necessarily yield better results, though further tuning experiments are required. Furthermore, we examine how probability density functions of precipitation intensity and the diurnal cycle over the central United States respond to increased resolution. We find that these metrics also improve, primarily due to more accurate representation of mesoscale convective systems which contribute a substantial portion of summer rainfall over the central United States.
